# A 54M-Parameter Model Matches LLM Query Fan-Out at 1/20th the Latency

Query fan-out — expanding a single user query into multiple sub-queries to broaden retrieval coverage — has become a standard technique in recommendation and search pipelines. The problem is cost. Running fan-out through a large autoregressive language model at inference time is slow, and running it multiple times (Best-of-N) to improve quality multiplies that cost. Most deployed systems either skip fan-out entirely or accept the latency penalty without a principled way to control what properties the generated sub-queries actually have.

A paper from the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, published in March 2026, attacks this from an unusual angle. Rather than optimizing the LLM that generates sub-queries, the researchers train that LLM with reinforcement learning to produce high-quality fan-out trajectories, then distill those trajectories into a 53.9M-parameter diffusion model that generates all sub-queries simultaneously in a single forward pass. The result is a system — R4T (Retrieve-for-Train) — that separates the quality optimization problem (handled at training time by RL) from the deployment problem (handled at inference time by the diffusion model).

Prior work on fan-out either treated sub-query generation as a prompt engineering problem or relied on Best-of-N sampling to improve quality, with no mechanism to enforce properties like diversity or semantic groundedness simultaneously. R4T is the first framework to compile RL-optimized behavior into a non-autoregressive retriever.

The number that frames the efficiency case: the autoregressive LLM baseline takes 1.46 seconds for a batch of 8 queries and scales to nearly 50 seconds at a batch size of 1024.

## The Efficiency Gap: Diffusion vs. Autoregressive at Deployment

The 53.9M-parameter diffusion model processes a batch of 8 in 0.07 seconds and a batch of 1024 in 4.21 seconds. Against the autoregressive LLM's 1.46 seconds at batch size 8 and approximately 50 seconds at batch size 1024, that is a consistent 10–20x speedup across the full deployment range.

The mechanism is non-autoregressive generation in embedding space. Rather than producing sub-queries token-by-token, the diffusion model generates all k retrieval directions simultaneously as a single tensor of shape k × d, where each row corresponds to one retrieval direction. The architecture — a Transformer denoiser with hidden dimension 1024, 6 layers, and 16 heads — is compact enough to substantially reduce deployment memory overhead relative to the LLM it replaces.

The autoregressive LLM's latency profile is dominated by high constant overhead at small batch sizes before scaling linearly. The diffusion model's profile is nearly flat at small batches and grows slowly — the practical consequence is that the speedup is not just a large-batch phenomenon. Even at batch size 8, the gap is more than 20x.

This efficiency advantage is the paper's most deployable result. But it comes with a prerequisite: the diffusion model only works because it was trained on synthetic supervision generated by an RL-optimized fan-out LM. The quality of the distilled model is bounded by the quality of the policy that produced its training data.

---

## Why Reward Design Is the Fragile Core of the System

The RL training stage uses Soft-GRPO — a variant of GRPO augmented with forward and reverse KL penalties to stabilize open-ended generation — with a composite set-level reward. The reward specification turns out to be the most consequential engineering decision in the pipeline, and the ablations make the failure modes explicit.

Training with only a groundedness reward — a nearest-neighbor distance penalty encouraging sub-queries to be close to actual database items — causes the policy to converge to degenerate, nonsensical strings. The paper's example: "line ending line ending line ending." These strings happen to minimize embedding distance to a specific database item while satisfying the reward without producing anything useful. This is a standard reward hacking failure, but the specific form it takes here — exploitation through repetitive token sequences in embedding space — is worth noting for anyone designing similar systems.

Adding an alignment reward (cosine similarity to the original query) without a diversity term produces a different collapse. The policy learns to simply paraphrase the original query, trivially maximizing alignment while ignoring semantic dispersion. Coverage doesn't improve because all sub-queries point at the same semantic region.

The composite reward — groundedness plus diversity via Vendi Score plus alignment — prevents both failure modes and produces stable training. But this means the system's behavior is highly sensitive to reward weighting. The authors acknowledge this without fully resolving it. The forward and reverse KL coefficients are set to 0.05; the group size for GRPO is 8; 128 samples are generated per query for supervision synthesis. These are the hyperparameters that determine whether training is stable, and they were tuned on specific datasets.

## The Quality–Diversity Tradeoff RL Doesn't Eliminate

The wide-scope coverage retrieval (WSCR) results reveal a tradeoff the system makes explicit but does not resolve. R4T-FOLM (Qwen) achieves the highest Recall@5K of 20.9 and Hit@5K of 64.6 among all methods — and the lowest Vendi Score of 27.5. The highest-coverage model is also the least diverse.

This is consistent with what RL optimization for reference overlap does: it concentrates retrieval around dominant semantic modes, the same modes that appear in the reference set. Coverage improves because the sub-queries converge on the right semantic regions; diversity suffers for the same reason.

The diffusion variants partially escape this. R4T-Diffusion (Gemma) achieves Recall@5K of 15.0 and Vendi Score of 46.2; R4T-Diffusion (Qwen) achieves Recall@5K of 16.5 and Vendi Score of 34.7. The distillation step appears to re-introduce some of the diversity inherent to diffusion-based generation — the model doesn't fully inherit the mode-collapsing behavior of the RL policy it was trained on.

Neither variant dominates on all dimensions simultaneously. The FOLM variant maximizes measurable coverage; the diffusion variant trades some coverage for broader semantic spread and substantially lower latency. The paper frames this as a genuine choice rather than a solved problem — which is accurate, and more useful than claiming otherwise.

One structural caveat: the reference sets in WSCR represent one plausible realization of query intent rather than exhaustive ground truth. Recall metrics are proxies for semantic coverage. A system that scores lower on recall but retrieves genuinely complementary items not in the reference set would look worse by this metric while potentially being more useful.

## Before Deploying Fan-Out at Scale

The paper's practical implication is not that diffusion-based fan-out is ready to drop into any retrieval pipeline. It is that the latency problem of LLM-based fan-out is solvable — but the reward specification problem is not something to defer.

Teams considering this approach should treat reward design as a first-class engineering problem before touching the distillation stage. The ablations show that naive reward combinations produce degenerate outputs, and the failure modes are not obvious from the reward definitions alone. Testing reward components in isolation, on a small dataset, before committing to full RL training is the minimum due diligence.

The coverage-diversity tradeoff should be decided explicitly rather than inherited from default settings. If the deployment context rewards breadth — surfacing items the user didn't know to ask for — the diffusion variant's higher Vendi Scores are the relevant metric. If the context rewards precision against a known reference distribution, the FOLM variant's higher recall is. These are different products.

The music dataset used in evaluation is proprietary, limiting reproducibility on that benchmark. The Polyvore fashion benchmark is public, and the query counts — 43,874 for OAR and 84,704 for WSCR — are large enough to be meaningful. But generalization to other retrieval backbones, modalities, and embedding spaces remains an open question the paper doesn't close.
