Dense Retrievers Already Know How to Be Sparse

Extracting BM25-Ready Vocabularies from Frozen Retrieval Models — No Sparse Training Required

Sparse retrieval methods like SPLADE are trained specifically to produce sparse representations — they use hard negatives, learned expansion objectives, and sparsity regularization to get there. Dense retrievers like Nomic or Contriever take a different path entirely, optimizing for dot-product similarity in a continuous embedding space. The assumption has been that these are two distinct approaches requiring two distinct training pipelines.

A new paper challenges that assumption directly. It shows that dense retrievers fine-tuned for retrieval already contain a sparse vocabulary in their activations — one that can be extracted with a sparse autoencoder trained on nothing but web text, then scored with BM25, and still match or beat purpose-built sparse retrievers on standard benchmarks.

The anchoring result: applying this method to an unfinetuned BERT yields only 0.245 avg nDCG@10 on BEIR. The same method applied to retrieval-finetuned backbones reaches 0.474–0.526. The sparse structure isn't in the architecture — it's learned during retrieval training.

The Setup: SAEs on Frozen Retrievers, No Retrieval Supervision

The method, which the authors call Latent Terms, works like this. Take a dense retriever and freeze it completely. Train a sparse autoencoder on its token-level activations using only a standard reconstruction objective over web text — 30 billion tokens from FineWeb-Edu. The SAE produces a 32,768-dimensional latent vocabulary. For any input, you get per-token sparse codes (16 active features per token), which are sum-pooled into a single sparse vector. Those latent feature indices are then treated as vocabulary terms and scored with BM25.

Nothing in this pipeline is retrieval-specific. No hard negatives. No sparsity regularization. No learned expansion. The SAE doesn't know it's being trained for retrieval — it's just learning to reconstruct activations.

The BERT control experiment makes the dependency explicit. Run the same procedure on a BERT model that hasn't been fine-tuned for retrieval and you get 0.245 avg nDCG@10 on BEIR. Run it on Nomic or Contriever — both fine-tuned for retrieval — and you get 0.526 and 0.474 respectively. The latent sparse structure is a product of retrieval training, not of the transformer architecture itself.

> 0.245 > avg nDCG@10 on BEIR when applied to unfinetuned BERT — versus 0.474–0.526 for retrieval-finetuned backbones — showing the latent sparse structure is a product of retrieval training, not architecture.

Retrieval Performance: Matching SPLADE Without SPLADE Training

On the BEIR benchmark across 15 datasets, Latent Terms+Nomic reaches 0.526 avg nDCG@10. SPLADE-v3 — trained explicitly for sparse retrieval with all the standard machinery — scores 0.502. The Nomic base model itself scores 0.521 using dense retrieval. So Latent Terms+Nomic edges out both the dedicated sparse retriever and its own backbone.

> Latent Terms+Nomic: 0.526 avg nDCG@10 on BEIR (15 datasets) > SPLADE-v3: 0.502 avg nDCG@10 on BEIR — trained explicitly for sparse retrieval

The pattern holds with Contriever, a model not originally designed for sparse retrieval at all. Latent Terms+Contriever reaches 0.474, beating SPLADE-v2 (0.450) and substantially improving over Contriever base (0.415). That's a model going from below SPLADE-v2 to above it, without any sparse training.

The one consistent gap is against MaxSim-based ColBERT. GTE-MC+Latent Terms scores 0.500 on BEIR, trailing GTE-MC base at 0.547. Late-interaction scoring expresses model knowledge that BM25 can't fully capture — when you collapse a multi-vector model into a single sparse representation, something is lost. That's a real limitation, and it's worth keeping in mind when choosing where to apply this.

Where Latent Terms Dominates: Single-Vector Failure Modes

BEIR averages can obscure where a method actually earns its keep. The LIMIT benchmark is designed specifically to expose cases where single-vector dense retrieval fails — and the results there are striking.

25×
Recall@20 improvement on LIMIT for Latent Terms+Contriever (0.5100) over Contriever base (0.0265) — a benchmark specifically targeting single-vector retrieval failure.

Contriever base achieves Recall@20 of 0.0265 on LIMIT. Latent Terms+Contriever reaches 0.5100 — more than 25 times higher. That's not a marginal improvement; it's a different capability class.

> 25× > Recall@20 improvement on LIMIT for Latent Terms+Contriever (0.5100) over Contriever base (0.0265) — a benchmark specifically targeting single-vector retrieval failure.

The high-k recall story is equally telling. GTE-MC base's Recall@1000 on LIMIT is 0.8795 — barely above its Recall@100 of 0.8720, meaning the model's recall ceiling is essentially hit by 100 results. GTE-MC+Latent Terms reaches Recall@1000 of 0.9775. The dense model plateaus; the sparse decomposition keeps climbing.

This is probably the most practically useful framing for Latent Terms: not as a general SPLADE replacement, but as a recall-expansion layer for dense retrievers that hit a structural ceiling at high-k. If your pipeline depends on first-stage recall before a reranker, this matters a lot.

Why BM25 Scoring Works: Zipfian Structure in Latent Space

BM25's IDF weighting assumes that term frequencies follow something like a Zipfian distribution — common terms get discounted, rare terms get amplified. For this to work, the vocabulary has to actually have that structure. The paper shows that the SAE-extracted latent vocabulary does.

~57% Broad topical features
~33% Lexical features
10% Narrow semantic features

When you plot term frequencies over MS MARCO for Latent Terms+Nomic, you get a distribution that's approximately Zipfian — pronounced high-frequency features at the top, then a long tail. SPLADE's distribution looks different: it lacks dominant quasi-stopword features, diverging from Zipf's law in a way that makes BM25 scoring actively harmful for it. SPLADE-v3 scores 0.568 with dot product but only 0.455 with BM25. The inverse holds for Latent Terms: 0.582 with BM25, 0.381 with dot product. The scoring method and the vocabulary structure have to match.

> Latent vocabulary composition > ~57% broad topical features > ~33% lexical features > 10% narrow semantic features

Automated annotation of the latent vocabulary — using Gemini 3 Pro with 500 manually reviewed samples showing perfect human-LLM agreement — finds that the majority of features are broad topical, roughly a third are lexical, and only 10% are narrow semantic. That distribution mirrors how classical IR vocabularies are structured, which is part of why BM25 works on them.

The feature distribution also explains the pruning sensitivity. Removing the top 1% of most frequent features drops nDCG@10 from 0.4095 to 0.4053 — small but consistent. Pruning 10% drops it to 0.3755. Those high-frequency features are doing real work as the IDF anchors of the vocabulary.

What to Do With This

If you have a dense retriever fine-tuned for retrieval and you want sparse representations from it, this paper says you can get them — without retraining the retriever, without sparse training objectives, and without hard negatives. Train an SAE on the frozen activations, score with BM25, and you'll likely match SPLADE-level precision while gaining substantially higher recall ceilings at high-k.

The practical ceiling is the MaxSim case. If your backbone is a ColBERT-style multi-vector model, Latent Terms will underperform the base model's late-interaction scoring. The method is best suited to single-vector retrievers where you want to add a sparse retrieval path — either as a standalone index or as a first-stage recall layer feeding a reranker.

Hyperparameter tuning helps modestly: tuned Latent Terms+Nomic reaches 0.536 versus 0.527 untuned, a difference of +0.009. Default BM25 parameters are already strong, so the method doesn't require careful calibration to be useful. The bigger dependency is on the backbone: the retrieval fine-tuning has to be there. Without it, the latent vocabulary isn't there to extract.

Key Takeaway

Dense retrievers fine-tuned for retrieval implicitly encode a Zipfian sparse vocabulary in their activations — one that SAEs can extract without any retrieval-specific training objective.

Practitioners can convert an existing dense retriever into a competitive sparse retriever using only an unsupervised SAE, gaining SPLADE-level precision and dramatically higher recall ceilings at high-k — at the cost of some performance on MaxSim-style tasks.
