Legal Article Retrieval Needs Structure, Not More General-Purpose Components

Why Stacking Better Retrievers Doesn't Solve the Problem

When practitioners build RAG pipelines for legal applications, the instinct is to reach for better components: swap in a legal-domain embedding model, add a reranker, layer on query reformulation. The assumption is that general retrieval quality, applied to legal text, will produce legal retrieval quality. A new paper from East China Normal University tests that assumption directly — and finds it doesn't hold.

The paper introduces LexPath, a multi-path retrieval framework built around the structural logic of Chinese statutory law. The finding isn't just that LexPath outperforms the baselines. It's why the baselines fail: they have no mechanism to exploit the hierarchical and citation relationships that define relevance in statutory law. Legal-domain embeddings like ChatLaw_Text2Vec and SAILER_zh don't consistently beat open-domain models like bge-m3. Hybrid retrieval with reranking and adaptive RAG produce inconsistent gains. Stacking general strategies cannot substitute for relevance modeling that understands what makes one legal article more relevant than another.

The number that anchors the paper: LexPath improves Recall@5 by 15.24 points over the strongest baseline on StatuteRAG — a 56,982-article corpus — while the best general-purpose approach leaves that gap essentially untouched.

The Problem with General-Purpose Legal Retrieval

The failure of legal-domain embeddings is worth sitting with. Document-level pretraining on legal corpora doesn't transfer cleanly to article-level retrieval. A model trained on full legal documents learns representations suited to document similarity, not to matching a user's question about a specific statutory provision against the right article in a 55,000-article corpus. The granularity mismatch is structural, and no amount of domain-specific pretraining fixes it if the training objective is wrong.

Hybrid retrieval — combining sparse and dense scores, adding a cross-encoder reranker — helps in general-domain settings because sparse and dense signals are genuinely complementary. In legal retrieval, the problem is different. BM25 without legal grounding matches surface keywords, not legal concepts. A query about liability for a defective product may use none of the exact terms in the relevant statute. Fusing a noisy sparse signal with a dense signal doesn't improve recall; it dilutes it.

Adaptive RAG, which reformulates queries or expands to multi-turn retrieval, runs into the same wall. Query reformulation without legal grounding just produces more surface-level variation. The core issue isn't query phrasing — it's that the retriever has no model of legal relevance: no sense that articles in the same hierarchy bucket are structurally related, no awareness that a cited article is likely relevant to the citing one.

How LexPath Models Legal Relevance

LexPath runs two retrieval paths in parallel, each designed around a different dimension of legal structure, then fuses their scores before reranking.

The sparse path uses an LLM to analyze the query through the IRAC framework — Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion — extracting legal keywords that get appended to the original query before BM25 retrieval. This makes lexical matching legally grounded rather than surface-level. The query isn't just reformulated; it's analyzed for its legal substance, and the expansion reflects that substance.

The dense path fine-tunes bge-large-zh-v1.5 using a hard negative mining strategy called Struct-Neg. Negatives are drawn from three sources: articles in the same hierarchy bucket as the positive (same type of law), articles at adjacent hierarchy levels, and articles that cite or are cited by the positive. These are the hardest negatives in legal retrieval — articles that look relevant by structure or citation proximity but aren't the right answer. Training against them forces the model to learn the fine-grained distinctions that matter in statutory law. The whole fine-tuning run takes under 0.5 GPU hours on a single A6000.

After min-max normalization, scores from both paths are fused via weighted linear combination. An intent-aware reranker then classifies both the query and candidate articles into five categories — Definition, Applicability, Consequence, Procedure, Others — and combines intent consistency with a neural reranker score. The intent classification uses few-shot LLM prompting.

Performance Gains Across Three Benchmarks

LexPath was evaluated on three Chinese legal benchmarks covering different retrieval conditions: STARD (1,543 queries, 55,348 candidate articles, open-domain legal queries), LexRAG (1,013 queries, 17,228 articles, multi-turn legal consultation), and StatuteRAG (1,361 queries, 56,982 articles, narrow domain focused on market supervision).

+15.24 StatuteRAG — largest gain, narrow domain corpus
+8.85 LexRAG — multi-turn consultation, harder recall ceiling
+7.43 STARD — broad open-domain legal queries

Recall@5 gains over the strongest baseline on each: +7.43 on STARD (reaching 56.31), +8.85 on LexRAG (reaching 32.45), and +15.24 on StatuteRAG (reaching 93.04). The pattern makes sense. StatuteRAG's narrow domain means the structural signals — hierarchy and citation — are densely informative, so a framework that exploits them gains the most. LexRAG's multi-turn consultation setting is the hardest: retrieval recall is harder to stabilize across turns, and the gains are real but smaller.

Downstream legal QA results across four generator model sizes confirm that the retrieval improvements carry through to generation. LexPath's retrieved articles produce better answers than those from BM25 or bge-large-zh-v1.5 alone, and this holds across model sizes — suggesting the retrieval quality difference is large enough to matter regardless of generator capability.

What the Ablations Reveal About Component Value

The ablation results are where the paper gets most useful for practitioners deciding what to build.

51.46
Recall@5 on STARD without the dense path (vs. 56.31 full system)
55.02
Recall@5 on STARD without IRAC-Exp — lower than removing the sparse path entirely

Removing the dense path causes the largest single drop across all datasets — Recall@5 on STARD falls from 56.31 to 51.46, on LexRAG from 32.45 to 22.49, on StatuteRAG from 93.04 to 84.98. The dense path, trained with Struct-Neg hard negatives, is the load-bearing component. Sparse retrieval alone cannot carry the system.

The IRAC expansion result is counterintuitive but important: removing IRAC-Exp hurts more than removing the entire sparse path. Without expansion, BM25 retrieval on STARD scores 55.66; without the sparse path entirely, it scores 55.66 as well — but removing only the expansion drops it to 55.02. An unexpanded BM25 path actively introduces noisy lexical matches that degrade fusion. The sparse path only helps when the queries are legally grounded.

The intent-aware reranker contributes the smallest marginal gain of the three components. Part of the ceiling is the classifier itself: LLM-based intent classification agreed with human labels 81% of the time on queries and 86% on articles — non-trivial error rates that limit how much intent consistency can improve ranking. For practitioners weighing latency against recall, the reranker is the first thing to drop.

Practical Constraints and Deployment Tradeoffs

Full LexPath takes 10.12 seconds per query on STARD. The dense path alone takes 0.30 seconds. That 34× slowdown comes from three online LLM calls — one for IRAC expansion, two for intent classification of query and articles. For interactive applications, that's a hard constraint.

34×
Latency increase from dense-only (0.30s) to full LexPath (10.12s) — the cost of three online LLM calls for IRAC expansion and intent classification.

The practical path forward is clear from the ablations. The dense path with Struct-Neg training is cheap to build (under 0.5 GPU hours), delivers the majority of the gains, and adds no latency beyond standard dense retrieval. IRAC expansion adds meaningful recall at the cost of one LLM call per query — manageable in batch or asynchronous settings. The intent reranker adds marginal recall at significant latency cost and should be treated as optional.

One scope caveat worth flagging: LexPath is built and evaluated on Chinese statutory law. The hierarchy and citation structures it exploits are specific to that legal system. Whether the same approach transfers to common law systems, other languages, or legal tasks beyond article retrieval is an open question the paper doesn't address. The structural insight — that legal relevance is defined by hierarchy and citation, not just semantic similarity — likely generalizes, but the specific implementation would need to be rebuilt for a different legal system's structure.

For teams building legal RAG on Chinese law today, the practical recommendation is straightforward: fine-tune your dense retriever with structure-aware hard negatives, add IRAC-guided expansion if latency allows, and don't expect general-purpose components to close the gap on their own.

Key Takeaway

General retrieval strategies — hybrid search, adaptive RAG, legal-domain embeddings — fail to consistently improve legal article retrieval because they ignore the hierarchical and citation structure that defines relevance in statutory law.

Practitioners building legal RAG systems should prioritize structure-aware dense training and legally-grounded query expansion over stacking general-purpose retrieval components; the structural components carry the gains, while intent reranking adds marginal value at significant latency cost.
