When Your Rewriter and Encoder Don't Speak the Same Language

Co-Training Closes the Gap That Independent Training Can't

Tool retrieval has a mismatch problem. A user asks something vague — "help me track shipments" — and the system has to find the right API from a catalog of 10,000 tools, each described in technical jargon the user never used. The standard fix is to put a language model in front of the encoder: have it rewrite the query into something that looks more like a tool description, then retrieve. HyDE does this. Query expansion does this. The problem is that neither approach trains the rewriter and the encoder together, so each component optimizes against a target the other component never sees.

CoHyDE, from researchers at SAP Labs, attacks that mismatch directly. Instead of training a rewriter and an encoder separately and then stacking them, it alternates between the two: retrain the encoder on the rewriter's actual output, then realign the rewriter against the encoder's actual scores, then repeat. Three rounds of this is enough to outperform any combination of the two components trained independently.

The gap is largest where it matters most. On well-formed queries, co-training adds about 2.5 percentage points of NDCG@5 over the best single-component baseline. On vague queries — the kind that omit domain-specific terminology and require the system to infer intent — the average gain is 6.3 points, with the hardest cross-domain tier gaining 8 full points.

The Mismatch Problem HyDE Never Solved

HyDE's logic is appealing: instead of embedding a short, ambiguous query, generate a hypothetical document that looks like what you're searching for, then embed that. The embedding space is denser and more uniform for longer, descriptive text, so retrieval should improve.

84.2%
Trained encoder used alone — HyDE combination makes things worse

It works — until the encoder has been trained on real queries. Once you fine-tune an encoder on (query, tool) pairs, it learns a representation space calibrated to raw query text. Feed it LLM-generated hypothetical descriptions instead, and you've introduced a distribution shift the encoder was never trained to handle. The result is that combining HyDE with a trained encoder actually hurts: G1 standard NDCG@5 drops from 84.2% to 73.4% compared to using the trained encoder alone.

Query rewriting without co-training is worse still on the hardest queries. On G3 vague NDCG@5 — the cross-domain split where queries are most ambiguous — a standalone query rewriter scores 6.2%, below the vanilla BGE baseline of 8.3%. A misaligned rewriter doesn't just fail to help; it actively degrades retrieval by pushing embeddings further from the catalog's representation space.

Prior retrieval-augmented systems — RAG, Atlas, REPLUG — train generators to produce better final answers. None of them close the loop by aligning the rewriter to the encoder it feeds. That's the gap CoHyDE fills.

How CoHyDE Closes the Loop

CoHyDE runs in alternating phases. First, the encoder (BGE-large-en-v1.5, 335M parameters) is retrained via InfoNCE contrastive loss — but exclusively on hypothetical descriptions generated by the rewriter, with no raw queries in the training data. The encoder learns what the rewriter produces, not what users type. Then the rewriter (Qwen3.5-4B with LoRA adapters) is preference-aligned via DPO, using NDCG@5 scores from the current encoder as the reward signal. The rewriter learns to produce descriptions the encoder can actually retrieve against.

Before co-training begins, both components go through a warmup. The encoder is fine-tuned on (query, tool) pairs with InfoNCE to give it a reasonable starting point. The rewriter is fine-tuned via SFT on the tool catalog across five rendering formats — title only, title plus API name, title plus description, and so on — so it learns the catalog's vocabulary before it ever sees a query. Skipping this warmup costs 9.6 points of NDCG@5 on G3 standard queries (50.5% vs 60.1%), so it's a prerequisite, not an optional step.

For each DPO round, the rewriter samples four candidate descriptions per query. These are scored by the current encoder via NDCG@5, and the highest- and lowest-scoring descriptions form a preference pair. After 4,371 optimiser steps, the rewriter has been steered toward descriptions the encoder ranks well — and in the next round, the encoder is retrained on those better descriptions, tightening the loop further.

Three rounds is enough. Performance is monotonically non-decreasing across five of the six evaluation splits from the start of training through round three. The single exception — standard G2 retreating by a marginal amount between rounds two and three — is the only non-monotonic cell in the trajectory.

Vague Queries Are Where Co-Training Pays Off Most

The evaluation uses a 10,000-tool subset of ToolBench with 1,092 queries across three generalization tiers: G1 (in-domain), G2 (cross-domain), and G3 (hardest cross-domain). Each query also has a vague variant — an intent-preserving paraphrase that strips out domain-specific terminology — giving six evaluation splits in total.

+8 pp
CoHyDE gain on G2 vague NDCG@5 (38.7% vs 30.7%) — the hardest cross-domain ambiguous query tier, where a misaligned rewriter would have made retrieval worse

On standard queries, CoHyDE's gains over the trained BGE encoder are real but modest: +2.6 pp on G1 (86.8% vs 84.2%), +1.9 pp on G2 (73.6% vs 71.7%), and +3.0 pp on G3 (60.1% vs 57.1%). Well-formed queries already map reasonably well to catalog embeddings, so the encoder trained on raw queries handles them adequately.

Vague queries are a different story. The trained encoder alone reaches 44.7% / 30.7% / 14.9% NDCG@5 on G1/G2/G3 vague. CoHyDE reaches 49.4% / 38.7% / 21.1% — gains of 4.7, 8.0, and 6.2 points respectively. The asymmetry makes sense: when a query omits the vocabulary the encoder was trained on, the rewriter's ability to infer and reconstruct that vocabulary becomes load-bearing. But the rewriter can only do that reliably if it's been aligned to the encoder's scoring, and the encoder can only recognize the rewriter's output if it's been trained on it. Neither component develops that joint capability alone.

Ablations: Both Components Must Be Co-Trained

The ablation table is the clearest evidence that co-training is the mechanism, not just better components. Four configurations are tested against full CoHyDE.

86.8% Full CoHyDE (R3) — G1 standard NDCG@5
63.2% Co-trained rewriter + vanilla encoder — representation mismatch persists
84.2% Vanilla rewriter + co-trained encoder — standard queries recover, vague queries do not
81.3% No rewriter warmup — cold-start rewriter degrades hardest tiers most

Pairing the co-trained rewriter with a vanilla (non-co-trained) encoder is the worst outcome. G1/G2/G3 standard NDCG@5 collapses to 63.2% / 38.1% / 36.2% — roughly 20–25 points below full CoHyDE. The rewriter has learned to produce descriptions calibrated to the co-trained encoder's representation space, but the vanilla encoder has never seen that distribution. The mismatch is larger than the original HyDE mismatch because the co-trained rewriter's output is even further from raw query text.

Pairing a vanilla LLM rewriter with the co-trained encoder recovers standard query performance reasonably well but leaves vague queries at 40.1% / 17.6% / 12.9% on G1/G2/G3 — up to 21 points below CoHyDE's 49.4% / 38.7% / 21.1% on the same splits. The encoder has learned to handle rewriter-style descriptions, but without DPO alignment, the rewriter doesn't know how to produce descriptions the encoder scores well on ambiguous inputs.

Removing the rewriter warmup before co-training drops G3 standard NDCG@5 by 9.6 points and pulls G1 standard down to 81.3% from 86.8%. Starting co-training with a rewriter that hasn't learned the catalog's vocabulary means the first round of encoder retraining is calibrated to poor descriptions — a cold-start problem that three rounds of co-training don't fully recover from.

What This Means for Tool Retrieval Systems

If you're running a tool retrieval system with a rewriter in front of a dense encoder, the practical implication is direct: the encoder needs to be trained on the rewriter's output distribution, and the rewriter needs to be preference-aligned against the encoder's scoring. Training them independently on the same data isn't enough — the distribution shift compounds rather than cancels.

The compute cost to reproduce the main result is around 50 GPU-hours on H200-class hardware, which is accessible for teams already running fine-tuning workloads. Three rounds of co-training is the reported sweet spot; the per-round trajectory suggests diminishing returns after that.

The gains are largest on vague queries, which is where most real-world tool retrieval fails. Users don't describe their intent in API vocabulary. A system that co-trains its rewriter and encoder handles that gap structurally rather than hoping the components happen to align. The 8-point gain on the hardest vague tier isn't a marginal improvement — it's the difference between a system that degrades gracefully on ambiguous input and one that doesn't.

One caveat worth keeping in mind: all results come from a single training seed on an English subset of ToolBench, skewed toward consumer-facing REST APIs. Whether the gains transfer to enterprise catalogs, non-English queries, or function-call schemas without free-text descriptions is an open question.

Key Takeaway

CoHyDE is evaluated on a 10,000-tool English subset of ToolBench with 1,092 queries; gains on vague queries are larger than on well-formed ones, and the single-seed training setup means per-cell confidence intervals on the smallest splits are ±4–5 pp.

When retrieval depends on a generative rewriter, training the encoder and rewriter independently — even on the same data — leaves systematic performance on the table; the encoder must be trained on the rewriter's actual output distribution, and the rewriter must be preference-aligned against the encoder's actual scoring, or the mismatch compounds rather than cancels.
