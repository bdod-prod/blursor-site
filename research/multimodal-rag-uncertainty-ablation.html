Multimodal RAG Needs Its Own Uncertainty Signal

Four Input Ablations, One Better Confidence Score

When a vision-language model answers a question, its confidence depends on at least two things: what it sees in the image, and what the retriever handed it. Standard uncertainty quantification methods were built for one or the other — not both at once. Drop a VLM into a retrieval-augmented pipeline and the existing UQ methods start to fall apart, because they have no way to separate "the model is uncertain because the image is ambiguous" from "the model is uncertain because the retrieved passage is irrelevant."

A new paper from Radboud University Nijmegen proposes LeMUQ — Learnable Multimodal Uncertainty Quantification — which handles this by running the model four times per query under different input conditions, then learning what the resulting probability patterns mean for answer correctness. The approach is straightforward in concept but requires white-box logit access and meaningful training time, so it's not a drop-in for every deployment.

The number that motivates the whole paper: image perturbation, a UQ method built specifically for vision-language models, averages 0.369 AUROC across all multimodal RAG test settings — consistently below random chance.

Why Standard UQ Breaks in Multimodal RAG

Image perturbation (Img. Per.) works by measuring how much a VLM's output changes when you corrupt or mask the image — the idea being that high sensitivity to the image signals high reliance on it, which can proxy for confidence. That logic holds when the image is the only non-parametric input. In a RAG pipeline, the retrieved context is also non-parametric, and the model's output can shift for reasons that have nothing to do with the image.

The result is that Img. Per. AUROC values range from 0.291 to 0.426 across all tested settings — not just weak, but inverted. A scorer below 0.5 is actively misleading: it ranks uncertain answers as confident and vice versa. That's worse than ignoring uncertainty entirely.

Even the strongest non-finetuned baseline, Predictive Entropy (PE), averages only 0.752 AUROC. That's a real signal, but it treats the model's output distribution as a single undifferentiated thing, with no way to attribute uncertainty to the image versus the retrieved context. The gap between 0.752 and a well-calibrated scorer is where LeMUQ operates.

> 0.369 — Average AUROC of image-perturbation UQ across all multimodal RAG settings, consistently below random chance

How LeMUQ Encodes Multimodal Uncertainty

For each query, LeMUQ generates four responses from the VLM: one with the full input (image + retrieved context + question), one without the image, one without the retrieved context, and one without either. Each response comes with token-level probabilities, which get discretized into quantile-binned vectors — a technique inherited from LARS, the text-only predecessor this work extends.

A finetuned RoBERTa-base classifier then takes all four probability encodings alongside the query, context, and response tokens, and learns to predict whether the VLM's answer is correct. The training signal is exact match against ground truth, and the output is a continuous score that can be used as an uncertainty estimate at inference time.

The ablation structure is the key design choice. By seeing how the model's token probabilities shift when the image disappears versus when the context disappears, the classifier can learn to distinguish image-driven confidence from retrieval-driven confidence. A single-pass probability estimate collapses both into one number and loses that distinction entirely. The ablation study confirms this matters: removing the no-context probability encoding and the no-image probability encoding causes larger performance drops than removing the other two components, suggesting those two carry the most discriminative signal.

The practical cost is real. LeMUQ requires four forward passes per query at inference time, white-box logit access to the VLM, and 10–27 hours of training depending on the hardware and retriever configuration — compared to 3–8 hours for LARS.

In-Distribution Performance Across Models and Retrievers

LeMUQ reaches 0.880 average AUROC across two datasets (EVQA and InfoSeek), two VLMs (LLaVA1.5-7B and Qwen3-VL-4B), and three retrievers (BM25, EVA-CLIP image retrieval, and BM25 with MiniLM reranking). The finetuned LARS baseline averages 0.843 over the same settings — a 3.8 percentage point gap that holds up under DeLong significance testing.

For context on what finetuning alone buys: the pretrained LARS baseline averages 0.730 AUROC. So finetuning on in-domain data accounts for most of the improvement over PE, and LeMUQ's multimodal ablation structure adds a consistent increment on top of that.

The gains are larger on Qwen3-VL-4B, where individual settings show improvements up to +0.086 over finetuned LARS. On LLaVA1.5-7B the gains are smaller — the largest individual setting improvement is +0.063. The authors suggest this reflects better probability calibration in Qwen3, meaning its token probabilities are more informative to begin with, giving the classifier more to work with.

> LeMUQ 0.880 vs. Best finetuned baseline (LARS) 0.843 — average AUROC within distribution

Where Generalization Holds — and Where It Breaks

LeMUQ generalizes well across datasets and retrievers. It outperforms baselines in 11 of 12 dataset-transfer configurations — training on EVQA and evaluating on InfoSeek, or vice versa. Retriever transfer is similarly robust: switching from the retriever used during training to a different one at test time doesn't substantially degrade performance relative to the baseline.

VLM-to-VLM transfer is a different story, and the asymmetry is striking. When training on LLaVA1.5-7B and evaluating on Qwen3-VL-4B, LeMUQ outperforms LARS across all six transfer settings. Flip the direction — train on Qwen3, evaluate on LLaVA — and LARS beats LeMUQ across all six settings.

> 11/12 dataset-transfer configs where LeMUQ beats baseline > 6/6 settings where LeMUQ wins on LLaVA→Qwen3 VLM transfer > 0/6 settings where LeMUQ wins on Qwen3→LLaVA VLM transfer

The likely explanation is that LeMUQ's RoBERTa classifier learns the specific shape of the training VLM's probability distributions — the quantile bins are fit to that model's output statistics. When the target VLM has substantially different probability distributions, those bins misrepresent confidence rather than capturing it. LARS, with fewer parameters and a simpler structure, is less sensitive to this mismatch.

This asymmetry has a practical implication: the direction of transfer matters, and you can't assume that a LeMUQ scorer trained on one VLM will generalize to another, even within the same model size range.

What to Do With This

If you're running a multimodal RAG system on open-weight VLMs and need reliable uncertainty scores — for routing, abstention, or human review triggers — LeMUQ is worth the training cost. The 3.8 percentage point AUROC gain over the best finetuned alternative is consistent across datasets and retrievers, and the method is straightforward to implement if you have logit access.

The constraint that matters most is VLM specificity. LeMUQ needs to be trained on outputs from the model it will score. If you're evaluating multiple VLMs or planning to swap models, budget for retraining — the Qwen3→LLaVA transfer results make clear that cross-family transfer without retraining is unreliable. The 10–27 hour training window is the real cost to plan around, not the four-pass inference overhead.

For teams using API-only or black-box VLMs, LeMUQ isn't applicable — it requires token-level probabilities. The PE baseline at 0.752 AUROC is probably your ceiling in that case, unless you can proxy logits through sampling.

Key Takeaway

LeMUQ was evaluated across two visual QA datasets, two open-weight VLMs, and three retriever configurations, with statistical significance testing on all AUROC comparisons.

Encoding how a VLM's confidence changes when the image or retrieved context is removed is a reliable signal for uncertainty in multimodal RAG — but the learned probability bins are VLM-specific, so cross-model deployment requires retraining on the target model's outputs.
