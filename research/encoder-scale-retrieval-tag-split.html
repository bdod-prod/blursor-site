Encoder Scale Doesn't Uniformly Win in Agent Memory Retrieval

Why the benchmark number you're trusting is probably wrong

Most agent-memory retrieval benchmarks hand you a single hit@k per retriever and let you draw conclusions from it. The problem is that number conflates two completely different sources of variance: lexical leakage, where query and distractor documents share entity tokens by accident, and tag-mixing, where structurally different query types — preferences, services, tools — get averaged into one figure. A retriever that's excellent at closed-vocabulary lookups can look equivalent to one that handles open-vocabulary intent queries, and you'd never know from the headline number.

This paper introduces an entity-collision protocol to pull those confounds apart. For each of five tag categories and five collision-degree levels, it generates controlled distractor documents that share entity tokens with the gold answer but flip the answer value — forcing the retriever to distinguish semantically, not just lexically. The result is a per-cell breakdown that shows exactly where each encoder wins, loses, or is indistinguishable from noise.

The anchoring number: on LongMemEval-S, the default MiniLM-based retriever reaches 0.810 hit@1 overall — but single-session-preference queries sit at 0.367. That 44-point gap is the recall cliff the paper spends considerable effort trying to close, and fails to close with any intervention tested.

The Confound Problem in Agent-Memory Benchmarks

The entity-collision protocol works by sweeping collision degree K across five tag categories: preference, project, technical, service, and tool. At K=1, there's one document in the corpus — the gold answer. At K=16, there are 15 distractor documents that share entity tokens with the query but give a different answer. Hit@1 measures whether the retriever still surfaces the right one. This is a much harder and more informative test than standard retrieval benchmarks, because the distractors are specifically engineered to exploit lexical similarity.

The tag categories matter because they represent structurally different retrieval problems. Service and tool queries tend to be closed-vocabulary: the query contains a specific entity name that appears verbatim in the answer. Project and intent queries are open-vocabulary: the query paraphrases a stored fact rather than repeating its surface form. Mixing these in a single benchmark number hides the fact that different encoders have fundamentally different strengths on each type.

Without this separation, you can't make a deployment decision. You just know that encoder A scored 0.72 and encoder B scored 0.74, and you pick B. The entity-collision results show that choice can be wrong in both directions depending on what your corpus actually contains.

The Two-Axis Retrieval Split: Lexical vs. Intent Tags

The clearest finding from the entity-collision sweep is that the three encoders divide cleanly along a lexical/intent axis — and that axis predicts performance better than encoder size.

18/20 BGE-large-1024 cells CI-positive at K≥4
5/20 HashTrigram-256 cells CI-positive at K≥4

HashTrigram-256 lifts on exactly two cells: service at K=16 (+0.057) and tool at K=4 (+0.141). All 17 other cells are non-significant. It's a zero-model-load option, but only when your corpus is template-bound — when queries use the same surface forms as stored memories. Under paraphrase conditions, the hash lift collapses entirely: at K=16 with varied templates, the confidence interval is [−0.006, +0.053], crossing zero.

ST MiniLM-384 is CI-positive across all 25 cells at K≥4. Its strongest lexical cell — service at K=16 — reaches +0.104, roughly 1.8× the hash lift on the same cell. It also holds up under paraphrase: the tool-tag lift grows from +0.043 under fixed templates to +0.096 under paraphrased ones, confirming that the semantic encoder is doing real work rather than exploiting surface overlap.

BGE-large-1024 is CI-positive on 18 of 20 cells at K≥4, versus MiniLM's 20 of 20. The two cells where it goes CI-null are both lexical tags — the exact cells where you might expect a larger encoder to hold its own. That's the first sign that encoder scale and retrieval quality aren't the same thing.

BGE-large's Asymmetric Win: Intent Up, Lexical Down

The direct BGE-large versus MiniLM comparison makes the asymmetry concrete. On intent-style project queries, BGE-large beats MiniLM by +8 to +14 pp at K∈{2,4,8,16}, all CI-positive. The semantic capacity of a larger encoder genuinely helps when queries paraphrase stored facts rather than repeating their surface form.

BGE-large gain over MiniLM on intent-style project queries (K=8)
−11.7 pp
BGE-large deficit vs MiniLM on lexical technical queries (K=8)

On lexical tags, the picture inverts. BGE-large loses to MiniLM by −2.7 to −11.7 pp across tool and technical tags. The technical tag deficit at K=8 is −11.7 pp with a confidence interval of [−16.4, −7.0] — a significant regression, not noise. A 2.7× larger encoder is actively worse on the queries where surface-form matching matters.

The latency cost makes this more than an academic point. On Apple Silicon MPS, BGE-large ingest runs at 21.5 seconds per instance versus 524 milliseconds for MiniLM — a 41× penalty. On commodity CPU, the gap is wider: roughly 150 seconds per instance for BGE-large versus 17–21 milliseconds for MiniLM. Deploying BGE-large uniformly means paying that cost on lexical queries where it either matches or loses to the smaller model.

On LongMemEval-S at n=500, BGE-large does recover an overall ∆hit@1 of +0.058 [+0.032, +0.086] versus MiniLM — a real improvement. But that aggregate masks the tag-level split. If your corpus skews toward intent-style queries, BGE-large earns its cost. If it skews lexical, you're paying 41× for negative returns.

The Routing Null and the Preference Cliff

The natural response to an encoder that wins on some query types and loses on others is to route: detect the query type at inference time and pick the right encoder. The paper tests this directly on LoCoMo, where there's a measured 11.7 pp gap between the static-best retriever and a per-query oracle that always picks the right vector weight.

0.367
hit@1 on single-session-preference queries versus 0.810 overall — a recall cliff no tested intervention recovers

Every routing signal tested leaves that gap intact. Tau-thresholded policies on raw gap, normalized gap, and crowd@0.95 signals all produce delta-versus-static-best confidence intervals that include zero. A leak-free GradientBoostingClassifier trained with LOCO-CV on n=1,978 questions doesn't recover the headroom either — it actually regresses, with ∆ = −0.0359 [−0.0566, −0.0152]. The oracle headroom is real and measurable, but no pre-routing signal tested can reliably identify which queries need which encoder.

The single-session-preference cliff on LongMemEval-S is the sharpest illustration of what's left unresolved. Hit@1 on those 30 queries is 0.367 against an overall baseline of 0.810. RM3 pseudo-relevance feedback doesn't move it — the paired ∆hit@1 is exactly +0.000 on all 30 instances. RM3 actually harms single-session-user recall by 7.1 pp [−14.3, −1.4], meaning query expansion is a net negative when the failure is semantic rather than lexical. Upgrading to BGE-large doesn't recover the cliff either. The preference queries fail because the answers are rarely lexically close to the question, and no tested intervention bridges that gap.

What to Do About It

The practical read is straightforward, even if the routing problem isn't solved. For closed-vocabulary corpora — agent memories that store service names, tool identifiers, and other template-bound facts — a 256-dim hash trigram recovers roughly half the dense-embedder lift at near-zero model-load cost. If your corpus is predominantly that shape, MiniLM is already more than sufficient, and BGE-large will cost you on the lexical queries that dominate it.

For intent-style queries — project context, user goals, paraphrased preferences — dense embeddings are required, and BGE-large's +8 to +14 pp advantage on those cells is real. The question is whether your query mix justifies the 41× ingest cost. If you're running on Apple Silicon or a CPU-only server, that's a concrete operational constraint, not a theoretical one.

The routing null is the harder message. There's 11.7 pp of headroom sitting in the oracle, and current signals can't reach it. That's not a reason to give up on routing — it's a specification for what a useful routing signal would need to do. Any signal that can reliably distinguish "this query paraphrases a stored fact" from "this query names a stored entity" would unlock real gains. Until that signal exists, the safer default is to know your corpus's tag distribution and pick a static encoder that fits it, rather than assuming a larger model covers all cases.

The single-session-preference cliff — 0.367 hit@1 on 30 queries that no tested intervention recovers — is probably the most actionable finding for practitioners building memory-augmented agents. If your application involves storing and retrieving user preferences expressed in natural language, the retrieval layer as currently understood is not the right place to look for a fix. The failure is semantic, and the path forward likely involves representation at write time rather than retrieval at query time.

Key Takeaway

The entity-collision protocol exposes that retriever rankings in agent-memory benchmarks are artifacts of tag-mixing and lexical leakage — and that a 2.7× larger encoder trades lexical precision for semantic range rather than improving uniformly.

Practitioners should route by query type rather than encoder scale: hash trigrams suffice for closed-vocabulary lookups at near-zero latency cost, dense embedders are required for intent-style queries, and no current routing signal can dynamically make that call reliably.
